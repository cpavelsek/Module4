Buying Used Cars


Purchasing your First Car

Buying a used car can be an exciting prospect, but it's essential to approach the process with caution to avoid potential pitfalls. A used car generally has a lower sticker price than a new one, and insurance costs may also be lower; high-end models also become more affordable as used cars, but used cars may have fewer features or older safety equipment, and many don't come with warranties. Before shopping, make a list of your needs, like cargo space, and desired features like heated seats, and check sites like Consumer Reports for safety ratings. Getting a vehicle history report from Carfax or AutoCheck is a good idea, and consider Certified Pre-Owned (CPO) vehicles from dealerships, which usually include inspections and warranties; CPO cars generally have less than 65,000 miles and are less than five years old.

Buying a used car can be a stressful experience due to a variety of factors, including high-pressure sales tactics, the fear of making the wrong decision, and financial concerns. Some dealerships may use high-pressure sales tactics to maximize profits, potentially leading buyers to feel rushed or make less favorable deals; these tactics can involve prolonging negotiations, urging immediate decisions, steering conversations toward pricier options, and exploiting end-of-the-month targets. The multitude of choices available, such as colors and add-ons, can overwhelm buyers and increase the fear of making a wrong decision, and salespeople can sometimes sway buyer’s decisions by offering a multitude of choices, creating an illusion of control for the customer. Recognizing these sales tactics and finding a car dealership known for honest sales practices are key to reducing the stress associated with the fear of making a wrong decision during the car buying process. Negotiating price, taxes, and fees, as well as understanding long-term financial commitments like car loans, can heighten the complexities and stress of purchasing a vehicle.

When buying a used car, securing insurance is a crucial step; in almost every state, you'll need to provide proof of insurance before taking the vehicle home from the dealership, as you can't legally drive the vehicle without the minimum car insurance coverage required by your state. If purchasing from a dealership, you must have proof of insurance and an active policy before leaving, and even when buying from a private seller where insurance isn't needed to complete the purchase, you cannot legally drive the vehicle in most states until it's insured. To get insurance, call your insurance agent, tell them you plan to buy a car, provide the VIN, and give them the start date, or if you already have car insurance, you can add the used car to your existing policy. You generally have a grace period of a week to a month to add the car to your policy, but it's best to do it as soon as possible to avoid coverage lapses. To get an accurate insurance quote, you'll need the car's Vehicle Identification Number (VIN).

Features and Options

When shopping for a used car, balancing safety, comfort, performance, and technology is key. Most cars have fundamental safety features like power steering, anti-lock brakes, airbags, and crumple zones; models after 2016 typically include a rearview camera. For advanced safety, consider features like lane-keeping assistance, electronic stability and traction control, seat belt sensors, road sign sensors, blind-spot detection, and pedestrian detection braking; blind spot monitoring uses sensors to alert drivers to obstacles in their blind spots, and lane-keeping systems help prevent a vehicle from drifting out of its lane. Newer cars (especially those made after 2014) often have LCD monitors or touch screens with Apple CarPlay and Android Auto and may also have voice-activated connectivity, wireless smart device support, USB ports, and multi-camera displays. Look for Bluetooth connectivity for hands-free phone use, and heated seats are a popular comfort feature, with remote starters being valuable in colder climates. If you plan to tow or haul, check the vehicle's payload and towing capacities, horsepower, and torque. A car with good gas mileage can save you money over time. A vehicle history report provides information about previous owners, accident history, mileage, and title status. Lower mileage usually means less wear and tear, but it's essential to balance this with the car’s age and condition; aim for a range that corresponds well with its age, for instance, a car driven less than 15,000 miles a year is often considered well-maintained.
